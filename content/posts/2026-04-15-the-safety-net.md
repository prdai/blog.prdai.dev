had a conversation with a friend about corporate life the other day. and it just... clicked. like, suddenly everything about my first experience working made sense.

## the first job

i joined my first company in december 2024. day one? genuinely terrified. but you adapt. people always do.

the first six months were rough. bad at it. properly bad. but by the end, something shifted. i wasn't great, but i was competent enough. the kind of competent where you can ship things without everything imploding.

what actually got to me was the people. seeing genuinely smart engineers work at that pace, produce at that volume — it's motivating in a way that's hard to articulate. you realize how much you don't know. it's humbling. but it's also the fastest way to actually level up.

the job gave me real memories. made me more mature. genuinely. there's something about being in a real organization that changes how you see things.

## the structure problem

after the initial buzz wore off, something shifted. the routine became... routine. same standup time. same sprint cycle. first week you're chill, second week you're underwater. it was fun, the team made it good, but the work started feeling repetitive in a way that made me want to do literally anything else.

it felt wrong. like, the structure was perfect. too perfect. everything in its place. and i felt completely out of place inside it.

## leaving

so i left. weird decision at the time. but then i had three months. three months where i wasn't working, wasn't doing corporate, just... existing.

the transition was genuinely disorienting. i couldn't sleep past my standup time for weeks because my body was literally programmed for it. but then uni started, and those three months became the best stretch i've had. rock climbing. random adventures. building random shit. the kind of freedom where you go and come whenever you want.

no regrets. not even a little.

## what actually mattered

here's what i realized talking to my friend: the whole time i was working, none of it was actually scary because i knew i could walk away. genuinely walk away. nothing bad would happen. and knowing that? that was the most comforting thing.

most people don't have that. they need the paycheck. the job hits different when leaving isn't an option.

and man, if you're an undergrad living at home, appreciate that. the freedom is insane. no expenses. no baseline to meet. you can just exist.

## the fear

i was supposed to go to japan in may. still not sure if i'm going, but one reason i'm considering not is this: if i went, i'd have to work to exist.

right now, existence for me is baseline. food, roof, everything i need. i'm happy. but the idea of relying on a paycheck for that? of needing a job to just... be alive? that scares me.

and it's coming. i'm going to a new company in june, probably. i'll still have the safety net for a while. but eventually it goes away. then you're paying rent. you're paying for food. you're reliant on that paycheck, especially if you have a family.

at that point, working on something you don't care about just because you need the money — that's the thing that actually terrifies me. not the work. not the structure. the dependence.

## the exchange

here's the thing: employment is just time for money. that's the exchange. but you want it to feel like more than that.

at first, it did. the growth was real. the challenge was there. but slowly, towards the end, it just felt like that was all it was. you trade hours for currency. and that's just... how it is.

i want to never get to that point. that's the goal. keep working on things that matter. keep it from becoming just a paycheck exchange.

these three months have been incredible. the freedom, the lack of schedule, the ability to do whatever. all of that disappears when you're working. and i'm not sure i can have this again without the safety net. three months off with no income isn't exactly sustainable once you're paying for everything yourself.

so right now, work is still a priority. but what happens when the safety net disappears and work has to stay a priority? no idea. probably figure it out when it happens.

but for now, i'm just living in this pocket of freedom. not taking it for granted. :))
