It's the cafe again. Random conversation with a senior engineer this time. I'm not gonna recap the whole thing because honestly I don't remember the specifics that well — but it kicked off a train of thought that's been sitting in my head for weeks. So here we go.

Recently a bunch of my friends started actually getting into the job market. And I've been watching what's happening to them.

## Two Kinds of Friends

I have a pretty decent variety of friends. But when I look at the ones who are job hunting right now, there's basically two camps.

One camp is what I'd call the modern programmers. They use the AI tools. Codex, Claude, whatever they prefer. They've integrated all of this into how they work.

The other camp is the more "traditional" devs. They love the code. They know their shit. They know their entire stack inside out. The dotfiles people. The ones who actually give a damn about the craft.

I'm not trying to do the whole AI vs non-AI thing here, that's a tired argument. I'm just using it as a way to split the group, because the split actually matters for what comes next.

## The Ones Who Got the Jobs

The people using AI tools got the jobs.

That's the observation. That's the thing that's been bothering me.

And the reason isn't really a mystery if you sit with it. The CV game right now is just different. It's mostly ATS scoring at this point. Some standardized matching workflow where it's not really a person reading your CV anymore — it's a basic check. Do you have these specific skills listed, yes or no. If yes, you're through. If no, fuck off.

A GitHub can kind of tell you something about a dev. Look at someone's repos and you can get a sense of how much they actually care. How much they're into this. ykwim? But a CV really can't portray any of that. Even when a CV could somehow signal it — maybe through the dotfiles of the dev linked in there — that's not really what's being matched on. Doesn't matter.

And from a company perspective, none of that matters anyway. They don't give two shits about your dotfiles or your "passion" or whatever. Do you have these specific skills? Yes or no. That's the actual filter. And honestly, from their side, it makes sense. It really does. It's a clean way to sort through thousands of applications.

There's a tension there though. The dev who really cares — the dotfiles person — gets a ton of satisfaction out of crafting their setup, knowing their stack, going deep. That matters to them. The company just doesn't care that it matters.

## The Bar Moved

Other thing I keep coming back to: the average project quality has gone up.

The bar for what counts as a decent junior project has shifted. And it's shifted because of AI. Not in a bad way, just as a fact. The average GitHub project is probably better now than it used to be. Sure, AI can spit out garbage too, ofc. But in aggregate? People are shipping more, and what they're shipping is more polished than what they would've shipped on their own.

Which means the bar for a "good junior level project" has gone up too.

And here's where it gets cooked. More polished projects in the pool, same number of jobs — probably fewer honestly — and you've got way more competition than before. From an individual and a company perspective it kind of all makes sense. From the perspective of trying to land your first job? It's weird. And it's brutal.

So now I'm stuck with this math problem. If I sit down and build a project without AI, putting in the hours, the days, the random side-quest thinking — it takes me a month. Someone else does the same thing with agentic tooling in a day. Why should I do it myself?

Like, the integrity, the dignity, the care — yes, all real. But at the end of the day, is that gonna get me that mf job I want? I don't think so. ;-;

## Doing It Anyway

Here's the thing.

So far I haven't really vibe coded a proper project and put it on my GitHub. I've used AI for shit like making this blog and other small stuff. But nothing I'd actually call a real agentic project shipped under my name.

I think "vibe coded" is a bad way to call it anyway. Agentic engineering, or whatever you want to call it. Same idea, less of the meme baggage.

But I've started one. My first actual properly agentic engineered project, where I'm basically just a reviewer. I read the code Codex and Claude produce. I review it. I steer it. I'm not the one writing it line by line.

And it has a really good tech stack. Terraform. EC2 instances. Stuff I have never worked with before. And it all fucking works. I'm shipping faster than I ever have.

So what's stopping me from putting that on my CV? Posting it on LinkedIn? A few more of those and I'm in fucking devops lmao. That's the path, atp.

## Catch Up or Don't

I'm leaning toward this approach.

I could probably agentic engineer something solid in Rust, in Zig, in whatever, in a matter of days. A really good project, even. And I think I can put it on my CV. Should I? I don't know. I've already been doing it for the small stuff — little open source contributions and such. That's fine, I guess. It just doesn't feel the same. The satisfaction isn't there.

But here's the part that actually gets me.

Those two types I was talking about earlier — the dotfiles people, the dev satisfaction people, the ones who know their shit. If they start using AI? I bet your ass they're gonna generate way better projects and code than the rest of us. Because they have the baseline. They have the instincts. They have the depth.

The baseline for quality and quantity has gone up across the board. And if you don't catch up, you're stuck in the bottom percentile. ykwim?

That's not it, gng.

I'll caveat all of this — I've been talking mostly about projects because we're in junior territory here. For senior people, their experience speaks way louder than whatever side project they have on GitHub. Some of this still applies to them asw I'm sure, but the pressure isn't the same.

Anyway. That's where I'm at. Contemplating. Leaning. Not fully convinced. We'll see. :))
